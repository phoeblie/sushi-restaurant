The Origins of Sushi

History

Sushi's journey began in China, not Japan, focusing on fermentation rather than raw fish. Initially a sour, street food staple in Edo (later Tokyo), sushi has evolved significantly over time. According to historian Eric Rath, author of *Oishii: The History of Sushi*, this dish quietly adapts in unexpected ways.

James Thomas

Sushi Critic

Sushi, a dish that has captivated palates worldwide, boasts a rich and intricate history that stretches back over a millennium. From its origins as a preservation method to its status as a global culinary phenomenon, sushi continues to inspire and delight, proving that great food is truly timeless.

Sushi's early history

One of the oldest forms of sushi is funazushi, made from aged nigorobuna fish and served on rice. This technique dates back to a Chinese dish called nare-zushi, which involved fermenting rice and salting fish for preservation, long before refrigeration existed. By the 8th century, sushi spread to Japan, where it gradually evolved with the introduction of rice vinegar.


The type most familiar to Americans—nigiri—emerged in Edo, largely thanks to street vendor Hanaya Yohei, who innovated by using fresh raw fish atop vinegar-seasoned rice in the early 19th century.

The Raw Revolution

Chefs were already exploring quicker sushi preparations when Yohei popularized nigiri. By the 1940s, many rural Japanese had yet to discover this style. After World War II, sushi began to gain popularity in urban areas, bolstered by increased interest in soy sauce and the introduction of a new, sticky medium-grain rice in the 1960s.

What About Nori?

Rolled sushi, or norimaki, likely originated in Japan around 1716, with techniques for creating nori—edible seaweed sheets—having been developed alongside similar Korean practices.

Sushi’s American Journey

Sushi began gaining traction in the U.S. after World War II, with influential restaurants leading the way. In 1967, New York Times critic Craig Claiborne noted a surge in sushi’s popularity in New York. Los Angeles’ Little Tokyo also thrived, with establishments like Osho attracting celebrities.


A growing health consciousness helped sushi become mainstream, especially after the U.S. promoted increased fish consumption in 1977. By the late 1980s, sushi had even found its way into major league stadiums.

The Future of Sushi

Sushi is likely to continue evolving, with suggestions like using sustainable Asian carp instead of endangered bluefin tuna. From its preservation origins to modern-day delicacies, sushi promises an exciting culinary journey ahead.
